The Labor Market Effect of South-to-South Migration: Evidence From the Venezuelan Crisis

with L. Bonilla-Mejía, L.F. Morales & L.A. Flórez · International Migration Review, Vol. 58(2), pp. 764–799, 2024

Published

Following Venezuela's economic collapse, millions have fled the country. This article assesses the impact of the migration wave on the labor market outcomes of migrants and nonmigrants in Colombia, the major recipient of Venezuelan refugees and migrants. We address potential endogeneity problems using a Bartik-type instrumental variable approach that exploits the regional variation of migrant networks and the timing and intensity of the Venezuelan economic crisis. While migration increases unemployment among migrants, it has no significant effect on nonmigrants' unemployment — migration significantly reduces the labor participation of nonmigrants, offsetting its negative impact on employment.

Is the COVID-19 Pandemic Fast-Tracking Automation in Developing Countries? Evidence from Colombia

with L. Bonilla-Mejía, L.A. Flórez, F. Lasso, L.F. Morales, J.J. Ospina & J. Pulido · Journal of Human Capital, Vol. 17(4), pp. 593–616, 2023

Published

We assess the effect of the COVID-19 pandemic on the automation process in Colombia, a country with low R&D and productivity and high labor informality. Using event-study models, we evaluate the differential effect of the pandemic on job openings and salaried employment by the potential degree of automation of each occupation. Both vacancies and salaried employment declined more in highly automatable occupations, though there is no evidence of persistent effects. Automation mainly affected the labor market of females, individuals over 40 years old, and low-paid occupations.

Effects of the Covid-19 Pandemic on the Colombian Labor Market: Disentangling the Effect of Sector-Specific Mobility Restrictions

with L.F. Morales, L. Bonilla-Mejía, J. Pulido, L.A. Flórez, K. Pulido-Mahecha & F. Lasso · Canadian Journal of Economics, Vol. 55, Special Issue, pp. 308–357, 2022

Published

We assess the effect of the COVID-19 pandemic and particularly sector-specific mobility restrictions on the Colombian labour market. Exploiting the sectoral and temporal variation of restriction policies, we find that mobility restrictions reduced employment, accounting for approximately a quarter of total job loss between February and April 2020. The remaining three quarters are attributable to the disease's regional patterns and other epidemiological and economic factors. Most of the employment effect is driven by salaried workers, while self-employment was more responsive to disease spread.

Venezuelan Immigration into Colombia, 2015–2026

Immigrant stock measurement · Labour market outcomes

Click to explore

Measuring the stock of Venezuelan immigrants in Colombia

In household surveys, immigrants are typically identified through retrospective questions. One of the most common is "Where did you live five years ago?" In Colombia, the largest inflow of Venezuelan immigrants occurred between 2018 and 2019 and then began to decline. This means that if we ask this question today (2026), a large share of the immigrants who arrived before 2021 goes uncounted. The graph shows this clearly: measured this way, immigrants peak at 3.9% of Colombia's population and then fade quickly to the current 1%.

A second approach is to use "Where were you born?", which captures Venezuela-born people. However, immigration from Venezuela also includes returnees: Colombians who emigrated to Venezuela during the 1980s and 1990s and came back in the 2010s. This question alone cannot capture them.

A third approach combines "Where were you born?" with questions about ties to Venezuela for those not born there; for example, whether the person ever lived or worked in Venezuela. The goal is to identify both the Venezuela-born population and people born elsewhere (including Colombia) who lived in Venezuela at some point and now reside in Colombia. In my view, this is the best measure of the stock of Venezuelan immigrants in Colombia. Unfortunately, the question about living outside Colombia was only introduced in 2021, so the resulting time series is very short.

I ruled out using citizenship, because Venezuelan immigrants with dual nationality tend to underreport their Venezuelan nationality in household surveys.

Given these constraints, I prefer the Venezuela-born definition: it offers a long time series, and although returnees were a significant share at the start of the wave, they ended up accounting for less than 15% of immigrants. This measure tells us that the peak of the Venezuela-born population as a share of Colombia's population was reached in 2022 with around 4.77%. It then started to diminish until the current 4%.

Venezuelan immigrants and their labour market outcomes

At the peak of the immigration wave in 2019, the unemployment-rate gap between Venezuelan immigrants and the rest of the population was about 5.5 percentage points. This gap was mostly driven by immigrants' higher labour force participation rate (LFPR): roughly 80%, compared with about 65% for the rest of the population.

After the pandemic, Colombia's unemployment rate fell to a historic low, and the gap between the two populations closed. The mechanical explanation is that immigrants' employment-to-population rate (ER) recovered to its pre-pandemic level while their LFPR did not. With less pressure from the supply side, immigrants' unemployment rate fell until it matched that of the rest of the population.

Nevertheless, the quality of immigrants' employment remains poor: the informality rate is around 76.5% for this group, versus about 51.5% for the rest of the population.

The 1918 Pandemic (Spanish Flu) in Bogotá, Colombia

Historical data reconstruction · El Tiempo newspaper archive

Click to explore

The 1918–1920 flu pandemic, popularly known as the "Spanish Flu," arrived in Bogotá, Colombia, in early October 1918. It was part of the second wave of the pandemic, which reached South America in the second half of 1918.

Daily registered deaths reached a peak of 148, and over the course of one month the city accumulated a death toll of approximately 1,568. At the time, Bogotá was a small city of about 144,000 inhabitants, representing a mortality rate of approximately 1%. For comparison, according to Bogotá's Secretary of Health, 15,503 people died in Bogotá in a single year of the COVID-19 pandemic¹; in 2020, the city had around 7.7 million inhabitants, meaning deaths accounted for approximately 0.2% of the total population.

During the 1918 pandemic, a large proportion of deaths occurred at home, due to the lack of adequate healthcare services, while the rest took place in the few hospitals the city had at the time.

The data was reconstructed from the El Tiempo newspaper archive, available through Google Newspapers. Since official public health records from this period are incomplete, digitized newspaper archives serve as a valuable, and often underexplored, source for historical research.
